Es necesario definir un umbral de aceptación para el rango potencial de la Probabilidad de Incumplimiento a un Punto en el Tiempo (PiT PD). Esto permite evaluar cómo los cambios en las variables afectan al PiT PD y asegurar que cualquier variación se mantenga dentro de un rango aceptable.

Definición del umbral de aceptación:
El umbral de aceptación puede definirse como un rango de variación permitido para el PiT PD, dentro del cual se considera que el modelo sigue siendo confiable y útil. Esto podría estar basado en los siguientes criterios:
1.	Histórico del PiT PD: Se puede definir un rango de aceptación basado en la desviación estándar del PiT PD histórico. Por ejemplo, si el PiT PD histórico oscila entre el 2% y el 4%, se podría establecer un umbral del ±1% alrededor de ese rango.
2.	Impacto en métricas de negocio: Otro enfoque sería definir el umbral según el impacto en las métricas clave, como las pérdidas esperadas (ECL). Si un aumento en el PiT PD del 1% produce un incremento significativo en las ECL que afecta la estabilidad del banco, entonces ese podría ser un límite de alerta.
3.	Comparación con benchmarks: Se podría establecer un umbral basado en el comportamiento de modelos similares o los límites regulatorios definidos por entidades como el Banco Central o el marco IFRS 9.
Evaluación del impacto dentro del umbral:
Una vez definido el umbral, los análisis de sensibilidad se deberían realizar evaluando el impacto en el PiT PD dado el rango aceptado. Por ejemplo:
•	Si el umbral es un incremento del 0.5% en el PiT PD, se evalúa si los cambios en las variables explicativas dentro del análisis de sensibilidad superan o no ese límite.
•	Se puede realizar una simulación para ver qué tanto varía el PiT PD al alterar cada variable por una desviación estándar, asegurando que la variación no exceda el umbral.

Definition of the Acceptance Threshold for the Range of the Point-in-Time Probability of Default (PiT PD)
The objective of this document is to define an acceptance threshold for the Point-in-Time Probability of Default (PiT PD) models developed for Argentina. These models are designed to improve the accuracy of PiT PD estimations and thus support the calculation of Expected Credit Losses (ECL) under IFRS 9, as well as Stress Testing exercises. The acceptance threshold plays a critical role in ensuring that the model’s outcomes remain within acceptable ranges, reflecting the inherent risk dynamics of each economic segment, and providing reliable results for both local and international regulatory frameworks.
Sensitivity Analysis and Segment-Specific Considerations
Based on the results from the sensitivity analysis conducted for the various economic segments, the following thresholds have been defined. These are grounded in the observed impacts of deviations in key macroeconomic variables on PiT PD, ensuring that model outputs remain within ranges that reflect realistic economic conditions.
1.	Segment 1: Primary and Food Manufacturing
For this segment, the sensitivity analysis considered macroeconomic factors such as government consumption, monetary policy rates, commodity prices, and total external debt. The analysis revealed that PiT PD increases by 0.8 percentage points (pp) when government spending decreases, and by 0.3 pp when there are negative fluctuations in commodity prices or external debt levels. Monetary policy rate hikes also resulted in a 0.5 pp increase in PiT PD.
Proposed acceptance threshold: Considering that the largest single-variable deviation is 0.8 pp, a threshold of ±0.5 pp is defined. This range allows for the normal fluctuations observed in these macroeconomic variables while ensuring that the PiT PD remains stable within an acceptable range for risk management purposes.

2.	Segment 2: Secondary and Construction
In the case of this segment, imports, the Merval index, U.S. short-term interest rates, and government external debt are key drivers. The sensitivity analysis showed that PiT PD increases by 0.3 pp due to negative variations in imports, the Merval, and U.S. interest rates. Additionally, government external debt fluctuations resulted in a 0.2 pp increase in PiT PD.
Proposed acceptance threshold: A threshold of ±0.3 pp is defined for this segment. Given that the observed variations are smaller in magnitude compared to Segment 1, this narrower threshold is appropriate, ensuring that PiT PD outputs are reflective of changes in market conditions while remaining within an acceptable range.

3.	Segment 3: Tertiary and Textile Manufacturing
For this segment, the sensitivity analysis examined the impact of long-term U.S. interest rates, Argentina’s GDP, Brazil’s GDP, and global oil prices. Negative variations in Brazil’s GDP, U.S. interest rates, and oil prices led to a 0.2 pp increase in PiT PD, while fluctuations in Argentina’s GDP had a larger impact, increasing PiT PD by 0.3 pp.
Proposed acceptance threshold: A threshold of ±0.3 pp is set for this segment, aligning with the observed sensitivity of PiT PD to macroeconomic changes. This threshold balances the need for model stability with the responsiveness required to capture economic shocks that impact these sectors.


Justification of the Threshold
The defined acceptance thresholds are based on the results of a rigorous sensitivity analysis across all segments. These thresholds reflect the model’s ability to handle reasonable deviations in macroeconomic variables without generating excessive volatility in PiT PD outputs. By aligning the thresholds with the observed impacts from key macroeconomic indicators, we ensure that the models are robust enough to capture economic cycles while maintaining reliability for credit risk assessments.
It is important to emphasize that, given the multivariate nature of the models, the total impact on PiT PD is not the sum of individual variable deviations. Each model accounts for the complex interplay between factors such as government consumption, interest rates, and external debt, ensuring that the cumulative effects on PiT PD remain consistent with real-world conditions.
Furthermore, the proposed thresholds provide a critical checkpoint for risk management. If PiT PD outputs exceed the defined thresholds, it may signal a need for further investigation, recalibration, or adjustments to the risk mitigation strategies. This approach also allows for proactive management of credit risks, particularly in stress testing scenarios where macroeconomic variables may exhibit greater-than-normal volatility.

Conclusion
By defining these acceptance thresholds, we create a structured and transparent framework for evaluating the output of PiT PD models. These thresholds ensure that the model remains sensitive to macroeconomic changes while preventing unwarranted deviations that could undermine its reliability. This method provides a consistent and measurable way to assess model performance under varying economic conditions, ensuring compliance with IFRS 9 and other regulatory standards.
